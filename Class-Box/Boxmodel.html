The Trojan War was a legendary conflict in Greek mythology that took place around the twelfth or thirteenth century BC. The war was fought between the Greeks and the city of Troy after Paris of Troy abducted Helen, the wife of Menelaus, king of Sparta. The war lasted for ten years and involved many famous heroes such as Achilles, Hector, Ajax, Agamemnon, and Odysseus. The story of the Trojan War is mainly described in two epic poems written by Homer: the Iliad and the Odyssey. According to mythology, the conflict began when Eris, the goddess of discord, threw a golden apple inscribed with the words "For the Fairest" among the goddesses Hera, Athena, and Aphrodite. Paris, a prince of Troy, was chosen to decide which goddess deserved the apple. Aphrodite promised him the love of the most beautiful woman in the world, Helen, so Paris awarded the apple to her. Paris later traveled to Sparta and took Helen with him back to Troy. Menelaus then called upon the Greek kings and warriors to help him bring Helen back. This led to the formation of a massive Greek army led by Agamemnon. The Greeks sailed to Troy and laid siege to the city for nearly ten years. Many battles took place during this time, and several heroes from both sides lost their lives. One of the most famous events of the war was the duel between Achilles and Hector, where Achilles defeated the Trojan prince. In the end, the Greeks used a clever strategy known as the Trojan Horse. They built a giant wooden horse and hid soldiers inside it. The Trojans brought the horse inside their city walls, believing it to be a gift. At night, the Greek soldiers came out of the horse, opened the gates of Troy, and allowed the rest of the army to enter. The city of Troy was destroyed, and the war finally came to an end. The legend of the Trojan War has inspired literature, drama, art, and storytelling for thousands of years and remains one of the most famous stories in Greek mythology.
